AI Analysis · Policy Finding

Alabama and Arizona share nearly identical child poverty rates — about 20% of children living below the poverty line. Yet Alabama graduates 91% of its high school students while Arizona graduates just 76%, a gap that poverty alone cannot explain. AI analysis of 16 states across the Southeast and Southwest identifies three policy levers that consistently drive this divergence: equalized state funding formulas that reduce reliance on local property taxes, compulsory attendance requirements through age 17 or 18 that keep students enrolled through critical years, and early adoption of science-of-reading curriculum. States that stacked all three outperformed similarly-impoverished states that pursued decentralized school choice expansion without those foundational investments.

Three Policy Levers That Explain the Gap
AI identified these variables by comparing states with similar poverty but divergent graduation outcomes
01
Equalized Funding Formulas
Southeast states with the strongest graduation rates — AL, TN, MS, NC — use state-level equalized funding formulas that reduce the impact of local property tax wealth on school resources. Arizona relies heavily on local funding, creating wide district-level spending gaps that compound poverty effects.
02
Compulsory Attendance Through Age 17+
Alabama, Tennessee, and Mississippi all require school attendance through age 17. Arizona's compulsory age is 16 — two full years earlier. Students who can legally exit at 16 often do so in high-poverty communities, before earning a diploma. That single policy variable correlates strongly with the graduation gap.
03
Early Reading Reform Adoption
Mississippi adopted science-of-reading curriculum in 2013 — a decade before most states. Alabama followed in 2019, Tennessee in 2021. Arizona adopted reading reforms in 2022, years after those states built foundational literacy infrastructure. Early readers complete more years of school and graduate at higher rates.
